## What it is
Gesture drawing is the practice of capturing the essential movement, weight, 
and life of a subject in a short time — typically 30 seconds to 2 minutes. 
It prioritizes energy and flow over accuracy and detail. It is the single 
most universally recommended practice for beginner figure artists because 
it builds observational speed, line confidence, and an intuitive feel for 
the human form simultaneously.

## Why it works
Short time limits force commitment. There is no time to second guess, 
redraw, or overwork a line. Every mark has to count, which rapidly builds 
the habit of intentional mark making that longer drawings require but rarely 
teach on their own. Artists who gesture draw regularly produce more confident, 
energetic work across all timescales.

## How to practice

- **Timer**: use a site like Line of Action, Quickposes, or SenshiStock 
  on DeviantArt. Set timers to 30 seconds or 1 minute to start
- **Volume over quality**: the goal is quantity — 50 bad gestures teach 
  more than 5 careful ones. Fill pages without judging individual drawings
- **Line of action first**: before any anatomy, draw the single curved 
  line that captures the dominant flow of the pose. Everything else 
  builds around it
- **No erasing**: commit to every mark. Erasing during gesture practice 
  defeats the purpose — the discomfort of a wrong line is part of the learning
- **Consistency over intensity**: 15 minutes daily produces better results 
  than 3 hours once a week. Build it into a warmup routine before longer 
  drawing sessions

## Progression
- **Week 1-2**: 30 second poses, focus only on the line of action
- **Week 3-4**: 1 minute poses, add basic body mass shapes around the line
- **Month 2**: 2 minute poses, begin suggesting limb placement
- **Month 3+**: mix timescales, add 5 and 10 minute poses for more developed gestures

## Common mistakes
- Drawing too carefully and slowly for the time limit
- Focusing on facial features and details instead of overall body flow
- Practicing only poses you are comfortable with — seek out difficult angles
- Stopping gesture practice once longer drawings improve — it should remain 
  a permanent warmup habit

## Exercise
Commit to 15 minutes of gesture drawing every day for 30 days before any 
other drawing session. Use 30 second or 1 minute timers exclusively for 
the first two weeks. At the end of 30 days compare your first session 
to your last — the improvement in line confidence and body intuition 
is usually dramatic enough to be immediately visible.
