## What it is
Line confidence refers to the quality and intentionality of individual strokes. 
Confident lines are smooth, deliberate, and placed accurately. Unconfident 
lines are scratchy, repeated, and hesitant — built up from many small strokes 
rather than committed single ones.

## Why it matters
Scratchy lines read as uncertainty to the viewer even when the underlying 
drawing is correct. A well proportioned figure drawn with hesitant lines 
will look worse than a slightly off figure drawn with clean, committed strokes. 
Line quality is one of the first things that separates beginner work from 
intermediate work visually.

## Why beginners struggle with it
The instinct is to approach a line cautiously — make a small mark, see if 
it looks right, add another. This produces chicken scratch. Confident lines 
require committing before you know for certain the line will land perfectly, 
which feels uncomfortable until it becomes habit.

## The ghost stroke technique
Before committing a line, rehearse it in the air above the paper several 
times without touching the surface. Feel the motion, find the arc, build 
muscle memory for that specific stroke. Then execute it in a single fluid 
movement. This is the single most effective technique for improving line 
confidence quickly.

## Grip and movement
- Draw from the shoulder and elbow for long lines, not the wrist
- A wrist-only grip produces short cramped strokes
- Hold the pencil slightly further back than feels natural to encourage 
  looser movement
- Work faster than feels comfortable — slow lines wobble, faster lines flow

## Exercise
Fill a page with single confident strokes — straight lines, curves, and 
ellipses — without lifting the pen and without going back to fix mistakes. 
Focus purely on committing to each stroke. Do this as a warmup before every 
drawing session for two weeks and notice the difference in your regular work.
