## What it is
Detail balance refers to the deliberate choice of where to place rendering 
effort in a drawing. Not every area deserves equal attention — concentrating 
detail at the focal point and simplifying everything else creates a natural 
hierarchy that guides the viewer's eye and makes the drawing feel intentional 
rather than exhausting.

## Why beginners overrender
The instinct is to finish every area of a drawing equally — leaving anything 
unrendered feels incomplete or lazy. In reality, evenly distributed detail 
produces visual noise. The eye has nowhere to rest and nowhere to focus. 
Selective rendering is a sign of control, not incompetence.

## The detail hierarchy

- **Focal area**: highest detail, sharpest edges, strongest contrast. 
  This is where the viewer should look first and longest
- **Secondary areas**: moderate detail, softer edges, slightly reduced 
  contrast. Supports the focal area without competing with it
- **Background and periphery**: minimal detail, lost edges, low contrast. 
  Recedes and gives the focal area room to breathe

## How detail directs the eye
The eye moves toward:
- Sharp edges over soft edges
- High contrast over low contrast
- Texture and detail over smooth flat areas
- Warm colors over cool colors (relevant when color is involved)

Placing the highest concentration of these qualities at your focal point 
and reducing them everywhere else is the mechanical implementation of 
detail hierarchy.

## Lost and found edges
Lost edges — where a form dissolves into the background or an adjacent 
form — are one of the most powerful tools for controlling detail balance. 
They allow areas to recede without being erased, and make the areas with 
found (sharp) edges feel more present by contrast.

## Common mistakes
- Equal detail everywhere — no clear focal point
- Heavy rendering in the background competing with the subject
- Sharp edges all the way to the frame edge — pulls the eye out
- Avoiding simplification because it feels unfinished

## Exercise
Take a reference photo and identify the single most important element — 
the focal point. Draw the entire scene but impose a strict rule: you may 
only use your finest, most detailed marks within a fist sized area around 
the focal point. Everything outside that area must be drawn with broader, 
simpler strokes. Compare the result to a version where you rendered 
everything equally.
