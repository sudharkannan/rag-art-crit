## What it is
Foreshortening applied specifically to arms and legs — the most common place 
beginners struggle with it in figure drawing. A leg kicking toward the viewer 
or an arm reaching out of the picture plane requires a fundamentally different 
approach than drawing limbs in a neutral side-on pose.

## Why limbs specifically
Arms and legs are the longest forms on the figure, so foreshortening 
compresses them the most dramatically. A fully extended arm pointing at 
the viewer goes from roughly 3 heads long to appearing as wide as it is 
long — a transformation the brain resists accepting.

## Key principles for limbs

- **Overlap is depth**: when the upper arm overlaps the forearm, the viewer 
  reads depth automatically. Draw the overlap clearly rather than blending 
  the forms together
- **Ellipses at joints**: the elbow and knee are cylindrical — when 
  foreshortened they show as ellipses, not pointed or angular shapes
- **Compressed proportions**: a foreshortened thigh may appear shorter than 
  the knee itself. Trust what you see, not what you know the length to be
- **Contour lines wrap around**: lines that cross a foreshortened limb should 
  curve to show the form turning toward the viewer, not run flat across it

## Common mistakes

- Drawing the full known length of the limb instead of the compressed 
  observed length
- Losing the overlap between upper and lower limb sections
- Drawing the joint as a flat hinge rather than a rounded cylindrical form
- Making the foreshortened limb too thin — compressed forms appear wider 
  relative to their length

## Exercise
Find three reference photos of figures with at least one limb foreshortened 
— a reaching arm, a kicking leg, a hand pushing toward the camera. For each, 
block in the limb as stacked cylinders before adding any detail. Focus on 
getting the overlap and compression right before worrying about anatomy or 
rendering.
