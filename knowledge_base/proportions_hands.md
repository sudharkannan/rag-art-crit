## What it is
Hands are one of the most challenging subjects in figure drawing. Beginners 
commonly draw hands too small, fingers too uniform in length, or avoid them 
entirely. Understanding the underlying structure makes them significantly 
more approachable.

## Key proportions
- **Hand size**: the hand from wrist to middle fingertip is roughly the same 
  length as the face from chin to hairline
- **Palm vs fingers**: the palm and fingers are roughly equal in length — 
  beginners frequently draw fingers too short relative to the palm
- **Finger length hierarchy**: middle finger is longest, index and ring are 
  roughly equal and slightly shorter, pinky is shortest reaching roughly 
  the top knuckle of the ring finger
- **Thumb**: reaches roughly to the middle of the index finger's first segment
- **Knuckle arch**: the knuckles form a curved arch, not a straight line — 
  the middle knuckle sits highest

## Why beginners struggle with it
Hands have many moving parts and no single simple shape to anchor them. 
The temptation is to avoid them, hide them in pockets, or draw them as 
vague blobs. The fix is to stop thinking of hands as a collection of fingers 
and start thinking of them as a palm block with finger cylinders attached.

## Construction approach
1. Block in the palm as a slightly tapered rectangle
2. Find the knuckle line — curved, not straight
3. Add finger cylinders in the correct length hierarchy
4. Add the thumb as a separate branching form from the side of the palm
5. Refine with knuckle landmarks and nail planes last

## Exercise
Trace your own non-dominant hand 5 times, paying attention to the proportional 
relationships. Then draw it from observation without tracing. Then draw it 
from imagination. Repeat this sequence daily for a week — your own hand is 
always available as reference.
