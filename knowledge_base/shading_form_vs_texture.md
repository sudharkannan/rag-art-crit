## What it is
Form shading describes the three dimensional shape of an object — where 
light hits it, where it turns away, where it falls into shadow. Texture 
shading describes the surface quality — rough, smooth, scaly, fabric. 
Beginners frequently render texture without establishing form first, 
producing drawings that look flat and busy simultaneously.

## The difference in practice
- **Form shading**: a sphere has a highlight, a midtone, a terminator line 
  where light turns to shadow, a core shadow, and reflected light at the 
  base. These zones follow the curve of the sphere regardless of what 
  material it is made of
- **Texture shading**: hatching, stippling, crosshatching applied to suggest 
  surface quality — scales, fabric weave, skin pores, rough stone

The mistake is applying texture shading before form shading is established. 
Texture without form produces a flat surface covered in marks rather than 
a three dimensional object with a surface quality.

## Form first, texture second
Always establish the major light and shadow zones before adding any texture. 
The texture should follow and reinforce the form — hatching lines that wrap 
around a cylinder rather than running flat across it, stippling that gets 
denser in the shadow zones.

## The five zones of light on form
1. **Highlight**: the point of most direct light
2. **Midtone**: the surface angling away from the light
3. **Terminator**: the edge where light transitions to shadow
4. **Core shadow**: the darkest part of the shadow, just past the terminator
5. **Reflected light**: light bouncing back into the shadow from nearby surfaces

## Exercise
Draw five simple forms — sphere, cube, cylinder, cone, and a rounded box. 
Shade each using only the five zones above with no texture whatsoever. 
Get comfortable with clean form shading before adding any surface detail. 
Then redo one of the forms with texture layered on top of the established 
form shading and compare the results.
