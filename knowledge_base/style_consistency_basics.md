## What it is
Style consistency means that the visual choices made throughout a drawing 
feel like they come from the same intentional place — the level of detail, 
the line quality, the rendering approach, and the degree of stylization 
all feel unified. Inconsistent style occurs when different parts of a 
drawing feel like they were made by different artists or with different 
intentions.

## Common inconsistency patterns

- **Detail mismatch**: a highly rendered face on a loosely sketched body. 
  The face gets all the attention while the rest of the figure is 
  underdeveloped, creating a floating head effect
- **Style mixing**: realistic hands on a cartoonish figure, or anime eyes 
  on an otherwise naturalistic face. Individual elements look fine in 
  isolation but clash when combined
- **Line weight inconsistency**: confident controlled lines in one area, 
  scratchy hesitant lines in another — not as a deliberate choice but 
  because the artist is more comfortable with some parts than others
- **Rendering approach mismatch**: smooth blended shading in one area, 
  hard cel shaded shadows in another, crosshatching in a third — three 
  different rendering languages in one drawing

## Intentional vs accidental inconsistency
Not all inconsistency is a mistake. Deliberately varying detail to direct 
the eye — high detail at the focal point, looser work in the background — 
is intentional and effective. The problem is accidental inconsistency 
driven by comfort level rather than compositional choice. Ask yourself: 
did I make this choice or did it just happen?

## How to develop consistency

- **Commit to a rendering approach before starting**: decide upfront 
  whether the piece will be loose gesture, tight line art, painterly 
  shading, or cel shaded. Every decision should serve that approach
- **Work the whole drawing together**: instead of finishing one area 
  completely before moving to the next, develop all areas at the same 
  rate. This prevents one section from pulling ahead of the rest
- **Use the same tools throughout**: switching between pen, pencil, and 
  brush mid drawing without intention produces inconsistent line quality
- **Reference your own work**: if you have a piece that feels stylistically 
  cohesive, keep it visible while working on new pieces as a consistency anchor

## The whole drawing at the same time principle
The most practical fix for style inconsistency is to never fully finish 
one area before roughing in everything else. Block in the entire drawing 
loosely, then develop all areas together pass by pass. This way every 
part of the drawing is always at roughly the same level of completion 
and inconsistencies are visible before they are locked in.

## Exercise
Take a previous drawing that feels stylistically inconsistent and identify 
which area feels most cohesive — the part that best represents what you 
were going for. Use that area as your reference point and redraw the 
rest of the piece to match it in detail level, line quality, and rendering 
approach. This trains your eye to recognize consistency before it trains 
your hand to produce it.
