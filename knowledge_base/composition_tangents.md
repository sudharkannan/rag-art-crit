## What it is
A tangent in composition occurs when two lines, edges, or forms accidentally 
meet or nearly meet in a way that creates visual confusion or flattens depth. 
Unlike intentional overlaps that show depth, tangents create ambiguity — the 
viewer cannot tell if the forms are touching, overlapping, or separate.

## Common tangent types

- **Edge tangent**: the outline of one form just touches the outline of 
  another — a head silhouette that exactly meets the horizon line, an arm 
  that just grazes the body edge
- **T-tangent**: one line ends exactly where another begins, creating a T 
  shape that reads as flat and awkward
- **Parallel tangent**: two edges run parallel and close together, creating 
  a visual channel that pulls the eye uncomfortably
- **Symmetry tangent**: an element placed exactly at the midpoint of another 
  — a head centered exactly on the shoulder line for example

## Why they happen
Tangents are almost always accidental. They occur when the artist is focused 
on individual elements without considering how they relate to each other in 
the picture plane. Something that looks fine in isolation creates a tangent 
when placed next to another element.

## How to fix them
The fix is always one of two options:
- **Separate**: move the elements clearly apart so there is unambiguous 
  space between them
- **Overlap**: push one element clearly in front of the other so depth 
  is established

Tangents live in the middle ground between these two — eliminate the middle 
ground.

## Exercise
Take three of your previous drawings and hunt specifically for tangents. 
Look for edges that just touch, lines that run parallel and close, and 
elements that sit exactly at the midpoint of another. Mark each one. On 
your next drawing, check for tangents before considering it finished — 
make it part of your review process.
