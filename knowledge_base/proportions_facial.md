## What it is
The face follows consistent proportional rules that beginners frequently 
violate, most commonly placing features too high on the head. Understanding 
these landmarks produces more believable faces regardless of style.

## Key landmarks
- **Eyes**: sit roughly halfway down the skull — most beginners place them 
  too high, forgetting the cranium takes up the top half of the head
- **Eye spacing**: one eye-width of space between the eyes, and one eye-width 
  from each eye to the edge of the face at eye level
- **Nose**: base sits halfway between the eyes and the chin
- **Mouth**: sits roughly one third of the way between the nose and chin
- **Ears**: top aligns with the eyebrows, bottom aligns with the nose base
- **Face width**: at its widest point, roughly two thirds of the head's height

## Why beginners struggle with it
The eyes feel like the most important feature so the brain places them 
near the top of the face. In reality the eyes sit at the midpoint of the 
entire skull including the cranium, which is much lower than intuition suggests. 
This single correction fixes a large percentage of beginner portrait issues.

## The three equal thirds
A useful breakdown of the face from hairline to chin:
- **Top third**: hairline to brow line
- **Middle third**: brow line to base of nose
- **Bottom third**: base of nose to chin

These thirds are roughly equal on an average face. The mouth sits 
one third up from the chin within the bottom third.

## Exercise
Draw an oval. Find the exact midpoint vertically and draw a light horizontal 
line — this is your eye line. Draw another line halfway between the eye line 
and the chin for the nose base. Divide the lower half again into thirds for 
the mouth. Place your features on these guides before drawing any detail. 
Repeat 10 times, then try without the guides.
