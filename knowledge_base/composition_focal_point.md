## What it is
A focal point is the primary area of interest in a drawing — the place the 
viewer's eye is meant to land first and return to. Beginner compositions 
frequently lack a clear focal point, distributing detail and contrast evenly 
across the picture plane so the eye wanders without landing anywhere.

## Why it matters
The viewer's eye follows contrast, detail, and edges. If those elements are 
spread evenly the eye has no reason to prioritize one area over another. 
A clear focal point tells the viewer where to look and makes the drawing 
feel intentional rather than scattered.

## How to establish a focal point

- **Contrast**: place your highest contrast — darkest darks next to lightest 
  lights — at the focal point. Contrast anywhere else in the drawing should 
  be lower
- **Detail**: render the focal point with the most detail. Surrounding areas 
  should be simpler and softer
- **Edge quality**: use hard, sharp edges at the focal point and soft lost 
  edges toward the periphery
- **Convergence**: use lines in the composition to point toward the focal 
  point — horizon lines, arms, architectural elements, gaze direction

## The single focal point rule
A drawing can have secondary points of interest but should have only one 
primary focal point. Two areas of equal contrast and detail compete for 
attention and split the viewer's eye, weakening both.

## Common mistakes
- Equal detail everywhere — nothing reads as more important than anything else
- Focal point placed at the exact center — static and uninteresting
- High contrast at the edges — pulls the eye out of the frame
- Multiple competing focal points of equal weight

## Exercise
Take a drawing you have already completed and squint at it. Where does 
your eye land first? If the answer is unclear or lands at the edge, 
identify your intended focal point and increase contrast and detail there 
while reducing it everywhere else. Redraw with that hierarchy in mind 
from the start on your next piece.
