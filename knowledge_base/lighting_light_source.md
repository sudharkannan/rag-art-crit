## What it is
A light source is the origin point of the dominant light in a drawing — 
the sun, a lamp, a candle, a window. Every shadow, highlight, and midtone 
in the drawing should be consistent with that single source. Beginners 
frequently shade each element independently without committing to a light 
source, producing drawings where the light appears to come from multiple 
directions simultaneously.

## Why consistency matters
Inconsistent lighting reads as wrong even to viewers who cannot identify 
why. The brain is extremely sensitive to lighting cues because we use them 
constantly to understand three dimensional space. A figure where the nose 
is lit from the left but the chin shadow falls to the left as well 
immediately feels off even if every other element is well drawn.

## Establishing your light source
Before shading anything, make one decision — where is the light coming from? 
Define it in specific terms:
- **Direction**: left, right, above, below, front, back
- **Height**: high angle, eye level, low angle
- **Distance**: close and intense or distant and diffuse

A simple note in the margin — "light: upper left, high angle" — keeps 
you consistent throughout the drawing.

## What the light source affects

- **Highlights**: appear on surfaces directly facing the light source
- **Cast shadows**: fall opposite the light source direction. If light 
  comes from the upper left, cast shadows fall toward the lower right
- **Form shadows**: the shadow side of a form always faces away from 
  the light source
- **Shadow edges**: sharp near the object casting the shadow, softer 
  further away
- **Reflected light**: bounces back into shadow areas from nearby 
  surfaces — always darker than the lightest midtone

## Common mistakes
- Highlights and shadows on the same side of a form
- Cast shadows falling in inconsistent directions across the drawing
- Every element shaded neutrally with no clear light direction
- Reflected light brighter than the lit side of the form

## Exercise
Draw five simple objects — a sphere, a cube, a cylinder, a cone, and 
a simple head. Shade all five under the same single light source — 
upper left, 45 degrees. Every highlight, shadow, and cast shadow must 
be consistent across all five objects as if they are sitting together 
in the same scene. This trains the habit of thinking about lighting 
as a unified system rather than object by object.
