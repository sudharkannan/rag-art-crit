## What it is
The rule of thirds divides the picture plane into a 3x3 grid using two 
horizontal and two vertical lines. Placing the focal point at one of the 
four intersections of these lines — rather than dead center — produces a 
more dynamic and visually interesting composition.

## Why it works
A subject placed at the exact center of the frame feels static and 
predictable. Slightly off center creates visual tension and gives the 
composition room to breathe — space for the subject to face into, move 
into, or exist within a context.

## The four power points
The four intersections of the thirds grid are called power points or 
crash points. Any of the four works as a focal point placement. Which 
one to choose depends on:
- Which direction the subject faces — place them so they face into the 
  frame, not out of it
- Where the secondary elements sit — balance the focal point against 
  other visual weight in the composition
- What mood you want — lower placements feel grounded, higher placements 
  feel elevated or unstable

## When to break it
The rule of thirds is a guideline not a law. Dead center placement works 
for symmetrical subjects, confrontational portraits, and compositions that 
deliberately invoke stillness or formality. Break it intentionally, not 
by accident.

## Common mistakes
- Placing the subject dead center out of habit rather than intention
- Subject facing out of the frame — leaving no space to face into
- Horizon line placed at the exact middle — splits the composition in half 
  with equal weight top and bottom
- Applying the rule mechanically without considering the overall balance

## Exercise
Take three reference photos and redraw the composition three times each — 
once with the subject at each of the four power points. Compare all four 
versions and identify which feels most natural for that subject. Notice 
how the mood shifts between placements.
