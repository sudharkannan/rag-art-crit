## What it is
Value structure refers to the deliberate organization of lights and darks 
across an entire drawing. Rather than shading each element individually, 
value structure treats the whole composition as a unified pattern of light 
and shadow. Beginners typically shade locally — each object gets its own 
shading — without considering how all the values relate to each other.

## Why it matters
A drawing with strong value structure reads clearly from a distance and 
guides the viewer's eye intentionally. A drawing with poor value structure 
looks scattered and muddy even if individual elements are rendered well.

## The three value approach
Before worrying about subtle gradations, simplify everything into three 
values:
- **Light**: everything directly hit by the light source
- **Midtone**: surfaces angling away from the light
- **Shadow**: everything in shadow, regardless of local color

Squinting at your reference collapses subtle value differences and shows 
you the three major zones. Getting these three zones right before adding 
detail is the foundation of strong value structure.

## Notan
Notan is a Japanese design concept meaning light-dark harmony. In drawing 
it refers to the abstract pattern of light and dark shapes across the 
composition. A strong notan pattern looks interesting and balanced even 
as a pure black and white abstract shape — no detail needed.

## Common mistakes
- Every element at a similar value — no clear lights or darks
- Too many value jumps — busy and scattered rather than unified
- Lights and darks distributed randomly rather than following a light source
- Rendering detail in shadow areas which should read as a single dark mass

## Exercise
Take a reference photo and squint until you can only see three values. 
Draw the composition as flat shapes of light, midtone, and shadow only — 
no gradients, no detail. This is your value map. Only add detail and 
gradation once the three value map reads clearly on its own.
