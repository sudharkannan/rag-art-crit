## What it is
Feet are among the most avoided subjects in beginner figure drawing, often 
drawn as flat blobs or omitted entirely. Like hands, understanding the 
underlying structure and proportions makes them far more approachable.

## Key proportions
- **Foot length**: roughly equal to the length of the forearm from wrist 
  to elbow — longer than most beginners expect
- **Foot height**: the foot is not flat — at the heel it is roughly one 
  third as tall as it is long
- **Ankle placement**: the inner ankle sits higher than the outer ankle — 
  a commonly missed asymmetry that makes feet read as flat when ignored
- **Toe length hierarchy**: big toe is longest and widest, toes decrease 
  in size toward the pinky — though this varies by individual
- **Arch**: the inner side of the foot has a pronounced arch that lifts 
  off the ground, the outer edge stays relatively flat

## Why beginners struggle with it
Feet are drawn too small and too flat almost universally. The brain 
simplifies them into a triangular wedge shape, losing the heel mass, 
the arch, and the ankle asymmetry. Shoes make feet even harder to 
understand structurally since they hide the underlying form.

## Construction approach
1. Block in the heel as a rounded box — this is larger than intuition suggests
2. Add the wedge of the forefoot tapering toward the toes
3. Establish the arch — the inner edge lifts, the outer edge stays grounded
4. Place the ankle landmarks — inner ankle higher, outer ankle lower
5. Add toes last as simple cylinders in the correct hierarchy

## Exercise
Draw your own foot from three angles — top, side, and three quarter view. 
Pay specific attention to the ankle height difference and the heel mass. 
Then draw a standing figure and consciously make the feet longer than feels 
natural — they almost always need to be bigger than your first instinct.
