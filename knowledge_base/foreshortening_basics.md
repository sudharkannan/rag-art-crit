## What it is
Foreshortening is what happens when a form points toward or away from the 
viewer. A straight-on arm looks like an arm. An arm pointing directly at 
the viewer looks like a short oval with a hand at the end. Beginners 
consistently draw the arm they know rather than the compressed shape they see.

## Why it's hard
Foreshortening fights against symbol drawing — the mental shortcut of drawing 
what you know a thing looks like rather than what you actually see. The brain 
knows an arm is long, so it draws it long even when the pose compresses it. 
Overriding this requires trusting your eyes over your assumptions.

## The core principle
Foreshortened forms show more overlap and less length. Instead of drawing 
the full length of a limb, you draw:
- A compressed silhouette that is wider than it is long
- Overlapping shapes that convey depth (the part closer to the viewer 
  overlaps the part behind it)
- Ellipses rather than long lines at the ends of cylindrical forms

## The cylinder test
Think of every limb as a cylinder. A cylinder viewed from the side is a 
rectangle. A cylinder pointed at you is a circle with an ellipse at the 
back end. Practice drawing cylinders at every angle until the compressed 
shapes feel natural — then apply the same logic to arms, legs, and fingers.

## Exercise
Draw 20 cylinders at random angles, some pointing directly at you, some 
at 45 degrees, some fully side on. Focus on the ellipse shape at each end — 
the more the cylinder points at you, the rounder the ellipse. Once this 
feels natural, replace the cylinders with simplified arms and legs.
