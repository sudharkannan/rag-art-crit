## What it is
Perspective is the system that makes flat drawings read as three dimensional 
space. Objects appear smaller as they recede into the distance, and parallel 
lines converge toward shared vanishing points on the horizon line. Beginners 
either skip perspective entirely or apply it inconsistently, producing spaces 
and objects that feel unstable or contradictory.

## Core concepts

- **Horizon line**: the eye level of the viewer. Objects above the horizon 
  line are seen from below, objects below it from above. Everything in the 
  drawing relates to this line — establishing it first is non negotiable
- **Vanishing point**: the point on the horizon where parallel lines converge. 
  The edges of a box, the sides of a road, the top and bottom of a building 
  all converge toward the same vanishing point
- **One point perspective**: one vanishing point, used when looking directly 
  at a flat face of an object or straight down a road or corridor
- **Two point perspective**: two vanishing points, used when viewing an object 
  from a corner — the most common perspective setup for figures in environments

## Why beginners struggle with it
Perspective requires establishing an invisible framework before drawing 
anything visible. Most beginners skip this and draw objects first, then 
try to make them look three dimensional after the fact. The result is 
inconsistent — some edges converge correctly, others don't, and the space 
feels unstable.

## Horizon line first, always
Before drawing any object in an environment, draw the horizon line. Decide 
where the viewer's eye level is. Everything else follows from that single 
decision. A figure standing on the ground will have their waist at roughly 
the horizon line if the viewer is also standing — this is a quick consistency 
check for figures in environments.

## Common mistakes
- No established horizon line — objects float inconsistently in space
- Parallel lines that don't converge — boxes that feel flat or broken
- Mixing eye levels — some objects drawn from above, others from below, 
  in the same scene
- Vanishing points placed too close together — produces extreme distortion

## Exercise
Draw a simple street scene using only boxes — buildings, ground plane, 
maybe a few rectangular objects. Establish your horizon line and two 
vanishing points first and don't place a single mark until those are set. 
Every edge of every box must converge to one of your two vanishing points. 
Repeat until placing the horizon line first becomes automatic.
