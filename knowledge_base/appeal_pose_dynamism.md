## What it is
Pose dynamism refers to the sense of energy, movement, and life in a 
figure's pose. Dynamic poses feel like they are caught mid motion — 
weight is committed, the body reacts to forces acting on it. Stiff poses 
feel frozen, symmetrical, and lifeless even when technically accurate.

## Why beginners draw stiff poses
Stiff poses are easier to construct — symmetrical, upright figures have 
predictable proportions and no challenging foreshortening. Dynamic poses 
require understanding weight distribution, overlapping forms, and the 
line of action, all of which take deliberate practice to develop.

## The line of action
The line of action is a single curved line that captures the dominant 
flow of energy through the entire figure — from head through spine to 
the point of weight bearing. It is the first mark in a gesture drawing 
and everything else is built around it.

- A straight vertical line of action produces a stiff neutral pose
- A curved or diagonal line of action produces energy and dynamism
- An S curve line of action produces elegance and flow

Draw the line of action before any anatomy. If the line of action is 
stiff, the figure will be stiff regardless of how well rendered it is.

## Contrapposto
Contrapposto is the classical figure drawing principle of opposing tilts — 
when the hips tilt one way the shoulders tilt the other to maintain balance. 
It is the foundation of dynamic standing poses and prevents the rigid 
parallel shoulder and hip lines that make figures look like cardboard cutouts.

- Shift the weight to one leg — the hip on that side rises
- The shoulders tilt opposite to compensate
- The spine takes a gentle S curve between the two

## Exaggeration
Dynamic poses almost always benefit from exaggeration beyond what the 
reference shows. Push the line of action further, tilt the hips and 
shoulders more, extend the reaching arm longer. What feels like too much 
at the drawing stage usually reads as just right in the finished piece.

## Exercise
Do thirty second gesture drawings using a site like Line of Action or 
Quickposes. For each figure draw only the line of action first — one 
single curved stroke — then build the figure around it in the remaining 
time. Speed forces commitment and prevents the careful cautious mark 
making that produces stiff poses.
