## What it is
Beyond technical consistency, lighting is one of the most powerful tools 
for establishing mood and emotion in a drawing. The direction, intensity, 
and quality of light fundamentally changes how a subject feels — the same 
figure lit from above feels heroic, lit from below feels sinister, lit 
from the side feels dramatic and conflicted.

## Why it matters
Two drawings of the same subject with identical technical execution will 
produce completely different emotional responses if the lighting differs. 
Lighting choice is a storytelling decision as much as a technical one. 
Beginners often default to neutral front lighting because it is easiest 
to render — but neutral lighting produces neutral emotion.

## Common lighting setups and their moods

- **Top lighting**: light from directly above. Feels natural, outdoor, 
  neutral to heroic depending on intensity. Strong top light with deep 
  shadows under the brow and nose reads as dramatic
- **Rembrandt lighting**: light from roughly 45 degrees above and to 
  one side, leaving a small triangle of light on the shadow side cheek. 
  Feels intimate, classical, painterly
- **Rim lighting**: light from behind the subject creating a bright edge 
  along the silhouette. Feels cinematic, separates the subject from the 
  background dramatically
- **Under lighting**: light from below. Immediately reads as sinister, 
  unnatural, or horror adjacent — because we almost never experience 
  light from below in real life
- **Flat front lighting**: light directly in front of the subject. 
  Flattens form, feels clinical or documentary. Rarely emotionally engaging
- **Side lighting**: strong light from one side leaving the other in deep 
  shadow. Feels dramatic, conflicted, morally ambiguous

## Quality of light

- **Hard light**: sharp shadow edges, high contrast. Feels intense, 
  dramatic, noon sun or bare bulb
- **Soft light**: gradual shadow edges, low contrast. Feels gentle, 
  overcast, diffused through a window

## Exercise
Draw the same simple head five times. Apply a different lighting setup 
to each — top, Rembrandt, rim, under, and side. Write one word describing 
the mood of each version before comparing them. Notice how much the 
emotional reading shifts with lighting alone and no other changes.
